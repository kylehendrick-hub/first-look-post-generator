# Writing Social Media Posts for Josh
## A Guide for Prachi (and AI Reviewers)

*Based on real feedback from Josh across 100+ draft/review cycles, December 2025 - March 2026*

---

## The One Rule That Governs Everything

**Write like Josh talks, not like a press release.**

If Josh wouldn't actually say it out loud to someone at a Capital Factory event, don't post it. Read every draft out loud. If it sounds like a corporate communications department wrote it, start over.

---

## The 10 Commandments

### 1. No Fluff. Ever.

This is the #1 issue. Fluff is any word, phrase, or sentence that doesn't add new information or make the reader feel something.

**Kill these phrases on sight:**
- "We're proud to..." / "We're excited to..."
- "We're honored to..."
- "It was great to..."
- "Big move for..."
- "Looking forward to continuing the work."
- "Appreciate the time" (don't repeat across posts)
- "Strong supporter of Capital Factory" (Josh would never say this)

**Prachi's draft:** "Big move for advanced manufacturing in Central Texas. Congrats to @Creative3DTech on relocating HQ & manufacturing to Cedar Park."

**Josh's feedback:** "Too boring / corporate"

**Josh's rewrite:** "It's too hard in California. First SpaceX in Bastrop, then X-Bow in Luling, now @Creative3DTech in Cedar Park."

*See the difference? The rewrite tells a story. It has a point of view. The draft is a press release.*

---

### 2. Shorter. Always Shorter.

If your post is more than 3-4 sentences, it's probably too long. Cut it in half, then see if you can cut it in half again.

**Prachi's draft (Neurable):** "One small step for man. One giant leap for how humans interact with technology. Consumer neurotech is here — starting with headphones that read cognitive signals to help gamers manage focus and stress."

**Josh's feedback:** "Too much hype not enough facts. Nobody knows what Neurable does."

**Josh's rewrite:** "Imagine if your computer knew how hard you were thinking? Neurable is working with HP and HyperX to make headphones for gamers that help to manage stress and focus."

*Cut the grandiose opening. Start with something specific that hooks.*

---

### 3. No Big Words

Replace jargon with plain English. If your mom wouldn't understand it, rewrite it.

**Prachi's draft (Surf Therapeutics):** "They've just launched their Early Feasibility Study to test it in patients. It could reshape how chronic inflammation is treated: fewer side effects, more precision, and a completely new therapeutic playbook."

**Josh's feedback:** "No big words. 'Early Feasibility Study' doesn't mean anything to most people."

**Josh's rewrite:** "Imagine treating rheumatoid arthritis without drugs and their side effects? Surf is using ultrasound to heal the nervous system and is just starting to test it in patients."

| Don't Write | Write Instead |
|---|---|
| Early Feasibility Study | testing it in patients |
| cognitive signals | how hard you're thinking |
| therapeutic playbook | treatment |
| innovation ecosystem | startups |
| regulatory friction | red tape |
| capital formation | raising money |
| workforce pipelines | hiring |

---

### 4. Lead with Attention, Not Context

The first sentence is everything. Don't start with what happened — start with why anyone should care.

**Prachi's draft:** "Kicking off CFHouse strong with Cup of Capital. Coffee, founders, investors and operators all packed into one room trading notes..."

**Josh's feedback:** "You're leading with the boring part."

**Josh's rewrite:** "Who do you need to meet? Cup of Capital is every morning at 9 AM during SXSW. Come find cofounders, collaborators, and investors."

The framework: **Hook → Facts → Call to action.** Not: Context → Description → Generic closer.

**More examples of boring openers vs. attention-grabbers:**

| Boring Opener | Attention Opener |
|---|---|
| "We started STATION DC to bring builders and policymakers..." | "STATION DC is bringing together innovators and policymakers in DC — are you on the list?" |
| "It's always interesting seeing what founders choose to build..." | "Most mental health diagnostics for kids look like tests. @AlmaPrism thinks they should be more like video games." |
| "@Casey Perley shared the @Army Applications Laboratory latest performance report today." | A specific, bold quote from Casey. |

---

### 5. Less "We" — More "You"

Frame posts around the reader, not Capital Factory.

**Prachi's draft (Station DC):** "We're building the future of American innovation from the heart of the nation, and opening membership to the next 250 members. Apply now."

**Josh's feedback:** "Less we — more you"

**Josh's rewrite:** "STATION DC is bringing together the innovators and policymakers in DC — are you on the list?"

Ask yourself: **Who is this post for?** Write to that person. Josh often asks: "What would the person we're tagging want us to say?" — a Congressman wants to look good to constituents, a startup wants exposure, a VC wants deal flow.

---

### 6. Specific > Generic

Every post should contain at least one concrete detail — a name, a number, a real quote, a specific product.

**Prachi's draft:** "Spent some time catching up with @SteveBrotman at the VC brunch"

**Josh's feedback:** "Doesn't make any sense with Steve Brotman. Nobody else knows the connection."

The fix: Context. Why does Steve matter? "Alpha has been investing in Capital Factory companies for 7 years." Now there's a story.

**The test:** If you could swap in any other person/company name and the post would still work, it's too generic.

---

### 7. One Thought Per Tweet

Don't cram multiple ideas into one post. If you have three things to say, make a thread.

**Josh's feedback:** "Disconnected. One thought per tweet."

**Thread structure:**
1. **Lead tweet:** Hook + the main point + photo + tags
2. **Reply tweets:** One company/person/quote per tweet
3. **Each reply should work as a standalone** — someone might only see one

---

### 8. Tag Aggressively

Every tag is an amplification opportunity. The person/company you tag might reshare.

**Always tag:**
- The person (full name or handle)
- Their organization
- The venue (@CapitalFactory, @STATIONDC_HQ)
- Anyone else relevant (interviewer, panelists, etc.)

**Josh's instruction:** "Post from me, then retweet me from CF. Comments are more powerful from people."

**Posting hierarchy:**
1. Post from @JoshuaBaer
2. Retweet/repost from @CapitalFactory
3. Same content on LinkedIn (both accounts)

---

### 9. Ask for Engagement

The best posts invite a response. Josh consistently pushes for engagement hooks:

- "Who should they meet while they're at SXSW?"
- "Reply and tell me who you need to meet and I'll try to connect you."
- "What are you building right now?"
- "How can we help them make some connections?"

**Don't just announce — ask.**

---

### 10. Use the GPT Every Time

Josh's #1 process instruction: **Run every draft through the Josh Writing GPT before sending it for review.**

But don't just paste in the AI output. The GPT gives you options — pick the best one, customize it, and make it yours. Josh's exact words: "Don't just give me the output — you pick the best one you like and customize. Then send me that."

And sometimes, ignore the GPT. It's a tool, not the boss.

## The Checklist

Before you hit send, run through this:

- [ ] **Would Josh actually say this?** Read it out loud in his voice.
- [ ] **Is there fluff?** Kill "proud," "excited," "honored," "great to," "looking forward."
- [ ] **Is there a hook in the first sentence?** Or does it start with context/description?
- [ ] **Can I cut 50%?** Probably yes. Do it.
- [ ] **Are there big words?** Replace with plain English.
- [ ] **Is it specific?** Names, numbers, quotes, real details.
- [ ] **Did I tag everyone?** Person, org, venue, related accounts.
- [ ] **Who is the audience?** Write for the person you want to reshare it.
- [ ] **Did I run it through the GPT?** Pick the best option, customize, don't just paste.
- [ ] **Is there a call to action or question?** Give people a reason to respond.
- [ ] **Is the image good?** Candid > posed. High res. Crowd/energy shots.
- [ ] **Would the person we're posting about want to repost this?** If not, rewrite.

- "Big move" / "Big deal" / "Huge"
- "Congrats to..."
- "Innovation ecosystem"

- "Looking forward to..."

## Timing and Process

- **Don't post at end of day.** Morning or midday is better.
- **Space posts out** 30 minutes to 1 hour apart when posting multiple.
- **Post from Josh first, then retweet from CF.**
- **Threads are better** than individual posts for multi-company events.
- **Get approval via text** for fast-turnaround posts. Send the full text, not a link.
- **Upload photos to CF Photo Inbox** in real time at events, not after.
- **Take lots of photos** — especially candid shots, crowds, energy. Live Photos when possible so you can pick the best expression.

## For AI Reviewers (Prompt Instructions)

When reviewing a social media draft for Josh, check for:

1. **Fluff detection:** Flag any instance of "proud," "excited," "honored," "great to," "looking forward," "strong supporter," or similar corporate filler.
2. **Length check:** If it's more than 3-4 sentences, suggest cutting.
3. **Jargon scan:** Flag any word that a non-expert wouldn't understand.
4. **Opening analysis:** Does the first sentence hook or bore? If it starts with context ("Kicking off...," "Starting the day with...," "Met with..."), suggest flipping to a question or bold statement.
5. **Audience check:** Who would want to reshare this? If no one specific, it needs to be more targeted.
6. **Tag count:** Are all relevant people and organizations tagged?
7. **Voice match:** Would Josh actually say this out loud? If it sounds like a press release, rewrite.
8. **Specificity test:** Could you swap in any other name and the post would still work? If yes, add specific details.
9. **Engagement hook:** Is there a question or call to action?
10. **"We" vs "You" ratio:** Minimize "we" statements. Frame around the reader.
